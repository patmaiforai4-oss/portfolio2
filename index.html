About

Hello, I'm Tangkwa — an architect who believes that great design begins with deep listening to context, culture, and the people who inhabit a space.

I graduated from the Faculty of Architecture, Department of Architecture, Kasetsart University (2020–2025), where I developed a strong foundation in conceptual thinking and abstract analysis as the basis for creating architecture that transcends aesthetics and functionality.

I am passionate about creating designs that meaningfully connect with the cultural and local identity of each place. Beyond architecture, I am drawn to interior design, event organisation, and spatial experiences that immerse users and encourage deep engagement.

Currently working as a Junior Architect at Creative Territories, where I continue to explore the boundaries between art, space, and narrative.

Creative Territories · Residential Renovation

Home Spa Ladkrabang

Site: Ladkrabang, Bangkok  ·  Area: 280 m²

→

A thoughtful renovation that transforms a residential house into a private home spa — creating a calm, Japanese-inspired atmosphere without sacrificing the home's original character.

Reclaimed timber from the original house is reused throughout as a design material, appearing in roof extensions, wooden walkways, and boundary fences. The material carries memory and adds warmth to the new programme.

The design choreographs a gradual spatial transition from exterior to interior through semi-outdoor corridors connected to a central courtyard. Natural light filters through to reinforce a sense of calm and relaxation. Sauna, pool, and massage rooms are arranged to create a flow of experience, while the residential programme above maintains privacy.

Creative Territories · Adaptive Reuse · Heritage

Prachoom Chang Project

Site: Si Phraya Rd, Bang Rak, Bangkok  ·  Area: 1,600 m²

→

A renovation of a nearly century-old building into a multi-disciplinary creative hub, housing three distinct identities — fashion (Philip Huang), furniture (Paribawga), and fine art (Channatip Studio).

The design philosophy is guided by "preserve and reveal" — the existing structure, walls, and marks of time are kept and expressed as they are. The original building becomes a living backdrop, allowing each tenant's craft and materials to speak clearly against it.

Architecturally, old and new materials are deliberately juxtaposed, creating a dialogue between past and present. Each zone maintains its own identity through materials, atmosphere, and function — separated for individual expression, yet connected for inter-disciplinary dialogue. The result is an honest space: minimal yet layered, where imperfection and ageing are embraced as beauty.

Thesis Project · Cultural Architecture

Suphan Buri Folk Music Center

Site: Chalerm Phatthara Ratchini Park, Suphanburi  ·  Area: 3,458 m²

→

A thesis project centred on preserving, promoting, and innovating Suphan Buri's living folk music tradition — recognised by UNESCO as a Creative City of Music.

The design envisions a civic cultural space where architecture mediates between past and present through areas for learning, performance, and musical creation. Curved structural forms generate a continuous rhythmic flow, echoing the cadence of folk music itself. Local bamboo and woven materials are employed throughout to reflect regional identity and craft.

The building programme includes a main performance hall, workshop and recording spaces, an exhibition gallery, food court, and outdoor performance stages. The site strategy connects the new building to the existing Banharn-Jamsai Tower and park landscape through a sequence of thresholds and gathering spaces.

Academic Project · Hospitality

Yodpiman Hotel Development

Site: Yodpiman River Walk (Pak Klong Market), Bangkok  ·  Area: 4,000 m²

→

A hotel design inserted into the context of Yodpiman Flower Market — one of Bangkok's most romantically charged urban settings along the Chao Phraya River.

The project addresses the challenge of building within an active market: preserving the existing character of trade and street life while creating a private, contemplative retreat. The concept keyword is "Romantic" — interpreted architecturally as a sense of interiority, warmth, and intimate connection to nature and craft.

The massing is organised into two interlocking zones — Live (hotel rooms) and Learn (market-facing retail and workshop spaces). Timber screens, open terraces, and water elements create a gradual threshold between the market's vitality and the hotel's calm. Standard and suite rooms step up over four floors, with restaurant and retail facing the river walk at grade.

Academic Project · Transportation Infrastructure

Bali Hai Terminal Station

Site: Bali Hai Pier, Pattaya, Chonburi  ·  Area: 7,800 m²

→

A monorail terminal station for Pattaya — a city recognised by UNESCO as a Creative City of Film. The project extends the station's programme beyond transportation into a cinematic experience space.

The design takes full advantage of Pattaya's dramatic hillside topography and coastal panorama. The station façade doubles as an open-air movie viewing screen, exploiting the axis-twisting structural geometry to produce distinctive visual angles at multiple vantage points across the city.

The route strategy connects Key landmarks (Walking Street, Bali Hai Pier, Pattaya Sign, cable car stations) into a cohesive transit and tourism loop. Environmental simulation across three seasons (summer, rainy, winter) informed the facade's kinetic screen system — polycarbonate panels modulate light and heat while maintaining spatial transparency.

Bangkok Design Week 2024 · Pavilion Installation

Rubberwood Frame Arch Pavilion

NRCT · Bangkok Design Week · Public Exhibition

→

A structural pavilion designed for Bangkok Design Week 2024, developed in collaboration with the National Research Council of Thailand (NRCT). The project explores rubberwood — an affordable, high-quality Thai material — as a medium for contemporary architectural structure.

The design uses a modular system of repeated units, each engineered for industrial production while forming an expressive free-form arch when assembled. The connection method relies entirely on interlocking geometry — no glue, nails, or mechanical fixings. Each unit's hinge capacity allows bending and folding, enabling the system to generate compound curves without additional connectors.

Two experimental iterations were developed: the first without overlapping interfaces (requiring adhesive), and the second with a nested, overlapping arrangement that achieves structural self-sufficiency through form alone. The final pavilion was exhibited publicly during Bangkok Design Week 2024 at the Livable Scape event.

Structural Journey 2023 · Workshop

Typological Interlocking Material

Workshop led by Prof. Kaoru Suehio, Kyushu University

→

A structural research workshop exploring typological interlocking as a construction logic — assembling complex three-dimensional forms from identical flat units without adhesives or hardware.

The workshop tested two approaches: a surface-alignment system (units side by side) and an overlapping nested system. The second arrangement proved structurally superior, enabling a dome form to hold without any joining agent. The hinge-like tolerance in each unit's slot allows the assembly to absorb compound curvature.

This work fed directly into the Rubberwood Arch Pavilion developed for Bangkok Design Week 2024, refining the logic from small-scale model to full building installation.

International Workshop 2023 · Urban Design

Wat Sai Floating Market — Re-zoning Study

With Kyushu University & BECAT University  ·  23–29 March 2023

→

An international urban design workshop held at Wat Sai Floating Market, bringing together students from Kasetsart University, Kyushu University (Japan), and BECAT University to analyse and reimagine a complex multi-programme riverside community.

The site encompasses residents, market vendors, temple visitors, students, and tourists — all layered over a dense network of water and land paths. Day-night analysis revealed underutilised time bands and disconnected zones that the design proposal sought to address.

The team proposed a 4-phase re-zoning strategy: re-connecting open and enclosed market areas through a new path network, introducing new activity programming to activate currently empty periods, and creating threshold spaces that draw visitors deeper into the community from the water's edge.
